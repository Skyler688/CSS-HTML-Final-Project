  • Description

    A forest biome is an ecosystem dominated by trees and other vegetation, typically with a higher amount of rainfall compared to other biomes. There are different types of forest biomes, such as tropical rainforests, temperate forests, and boreal forests (taigas). These forests are home to a wide variety of plant and animal species, with many animals living in the canopy or on the forest floor. Forests help regulate the climate by absorbing carbon dioxide and producing oxygen, and they play a key role in the water cycle. Forests are found in regions with moderate to high rainfall and can vary in temperature depending on the type.

  • Inhabitants

    In forest biomes, you'll find a wide variety of both plants and animals, each adapted to their specific environment. In temperate forests, you might spot animals like deer grazing through the underbrush, bears foraging for food, and squirrels darting through the trees. These forests often have towering oak trees and lush ferns covering the forest floor. In boreal forests (or taigas), animals like wolves and moose roam beneath pine trees, while the forest floor is often carpeted with mosses and low shrubs. In the tropical rainforests, monkeys swing from the dense canopy, and insects such as colorful butterflies and beetles buzz about. Tall, broad-leaved plants like ferns and tropical flowers thrive here, while cacti may be found in tropical dry forests, where rainfall is less frequent. Each of these plants and animals plays a vital role in maintaining the balance of the forest ecosystem.
