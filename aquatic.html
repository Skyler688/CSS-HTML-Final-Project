Description

An aquatic biome is an ecosystem found in water, either in oceans, lakes, rivers, or wetlands. It is characterized by the presence of water as the primary habitat for plant and animal life. Aquatic biomes are divided into two main types: marine (saltwater) and freshwater (rivers, lakes, and ponds). Marine biomes cover much of the Earth's surface and include ecosystems like coral reefs and open oceans, while freshwater biomes are smaller and include rivers, lakes, and wetlands. Both types are rich in biodiversity, supporting a wide range of species, from tiny plankton to large whales or freshwater fish. The environment in an aquatic biome can vary in terms of temperature, light, and salinity, influencing the types of organisms that can live there.

Inhabitants

In marine aquatic biomes, you'll find animals like fish (such as clownfish and tuna), sharks, whales, and sea turtles. Jellyfish, octopuses, and crabs are also common in oceans. As for plants, kelp forests are a major feature, along with sea grasses and phytoplankton, which are essential for marine food chains.

In freshwater aquatic biomes, common animals include frogs, freshwater fish like bass and trout, beavers, and ducks. Plants in freshwater environments include water lilies, cattails, and duckweed, which float on the surface of ponds, lakes, and rivers. These plants help provide oxygen and shelter for aquatic creatures.
