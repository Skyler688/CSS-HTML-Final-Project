  • Description

    The tundra biome is characterized by extremely cold temperatures, with long winters and short, cool summers. The environment is generally flat and barren, often marked by vast, open spaces. The soil is typically frozen year-round, known as permafrost, which limits the types of plants that can grow. Because of the low temperatures and limited sunlight, there's very little evaporation, so the air tends to be dry. Snow and ice are common, and in some areas, the ground may remain snow-covered for much of the year. The tundra's harsh conditions make it one of the most extreme and inhospitable environments on Earth.

  • Inhabitants

    In the tundra biome, life is tough, but a variety of plants and animals have adapted to survive the extreme cold and short growing seasons. The animal life includes creatures like the arctic fox, with its thick white fur that helps it blend into the snowy landscape, and the caribou, which migrates across the vast, frozen terrain. Polar bears roam the icy expanses, hunting seals, while snowy owls blend into the winter environment, hunting small mammals. Small rodents, such as lemmings, burrow into the snow for shelter, contributing to the food chain in this frigid environment.

    Plant life is just as adapted to the cold. Mosses and lichens are some of the few plants that can survive in the nutrient-poor, frozen soil, while hardy dwarf shrubs like willows and birches grow close to the ground to withstand harsh winds and temperatures. In the summer, the thawed soil allows grasses and sedges to grow in tufts or clumps, adding some green to the otherwise barren landscape. Despite the harsh conditions, these plants and animals have evolved unique ways to thrive in the challenging tundra environment.
