  • Description

    A desert biome is a type of ecosystem characterized by dry, arid conditions with very little rainfall—typically less than 10 inches (25 cm) a year. Deserts can be hot, like the Sahara, or cold, like the Gobi. Vegetation is sparse due to the lack of water, and the flora and fauna are adapted to conserve moisture and survive extreme temperature fluctuations.

  • Inhabitants

    In the desert biome, animals have special adaptations to survive extreme heat and dryness. Camels can go without water for long periods, storing fat in their humps for energy. The fennec fox has large ears that help regulate body temperature and is nocturnal to avoid the heat. Rattlesnakes use camouflage and venom to hunt small animals. Desert locusts can survive extreme conditions, often burrowing to escape the heat. The horned lizard blends into the sand and can squirt blood from its eyes as a defense. These animals have unique ways of conserving water and staying cool in the harsh desert environment.

    Plants in the desert are also adapted to survive with little water. Cacti store water in their thick stems and have spines instead of leaves to reduce water loss. Creosote bushes have small, waxy leaves that help retain moisture. Joshua trees have deep roots to access underground water and tough leaves to minimize evaporation. Sagebrush is another hardy plant, with a strong aroma and small leaves that help it survive the desert's heat and dryness.
